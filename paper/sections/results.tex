\label{sec:numerical-examples}

This section turns from problem definition to computational behavior. The
benchmark families in \Cref{sec:benchmarks} established the governing
equations, discrete energies, and representative states. Here we focus on
parallel timings, scaling trends, and the comparative cost of alternative
solver or discretization choices. We distinguish matched comparisons from
historical or context-only evidence. Matched comparisons keep the problem
contract fixed across implementations or derivative routes; historical evidence
is retained when it is still informative for workflow viability, but it is
labeled as such rather than merged into the matched-comparison story.

\subsection{Plasticity3D}
\label{sec:num-plasticity3d}

The governing problem, Davis-B reduction, scalar constitutive potential, and
deviatoric-strain norm are defined in
\Cref{sec:bench-plasticity3d,eq:plasticity3d-discrete,eq:plasticity3d-davis,eq:plasticity3d-potential,eq:plasticity3d-devnorm}.
This family has to be read in three layers: validation of the maintained
surrogate against source-family references, derivative-route choice on one locked
flagship case, and only then maintained discretization and historical scaling
evidence.

\paragraph{Validation.}
The first question is not scaling but interpretation. Because
\Cref{sec:bench-plasticity3d} defines an algorithmic endpoint surrogate, the
paper separates same-case source-faithfulness from a fixed-lambda
source-operator comparison. The same-case direct-branch comparison on the
glued-bottom \code{P2(L1)} case is tight: the layer-1A work relative
difference is $3.877\times10^{-5}$, the displacement relative $L^2$
difference is $3.517\times10^{-3}$, and the deviatoric-strain relative $L^2$
difference is $8.720\times10^{-3}$. The fixed-lambda source-operator layer
keeps the highest-successful-$\lambda$ schedule proxy aligned on the tested
grid, but it does not satisfy the stricter field-agreement thresholds: the
$u_{\max}(\lambda)$ relative $L^2$ difference is $1.756\times10^{-1}$, the
endpoint displacement relative $L^2$ difference is $5.295\times10^{-1}$, and
the boundary-profile relative $L^2$ difference is $5.065\times10^{-1}$.

\paragraph{Derivative-route ablation.}
With that interpretation fixed, the next question is which second-order route
has the most useful cost-quality profile for the promoted maintained workflow. On the locked
\code{P4(L1)} case at $\lambda_{\mathrm{sr}}=1.5$ and 8 MPI ranks, all three
routes converge to essentially the same terminal state, with identical Newton
and total Krylov counts. The differences are therefore cost differences, not
state-quality differences: constitutive AD has the lowest median wall time on
this locked case, with median wall time \SI{221.689}{s} and median solve time
\SI{190.503}{s}, compared with \SI{288.021}{s}/\SI{254.879}{s} for element AD
and \SI{371.993}{s}/\SI{252.814}{s} for colored SFD.
